pitch: >-
  This is the fattoush that made Za'atar on Cesar Chavez worth the trip — a
  Levantine bread salad built for crunch and tang. Torn pita gets tossed in olive
  oil and sumac and baked until it shatters, going in at the last second so it
  drinks the dressing without going soft. The dressing is the restaurant's tell:
  fresh lemon, grassy za'atar, tart sumac, and a whisper of pomegranate molasses,
  poured over arugula, cherry tomatoes, cucumber, feta, and kalamata. *The secret
  weapon is a bowl of quick-pickled radishes — ten minutes in vinegar turns them
  hot-pink and puckery, and they cut the richness like nothing else.*

ingredients:
  - section: Crispy pita chips
    items:
      - 2 pita breads, split and torn into bite-size pieces
      - 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
      - ½ tsp sumac
      - ¼ tsp kosher salt
  - section: Quick-pickled radishes
    items:
      - 6 radishes, very thinly sliced
      - ⅓ cup red wine vinegar
      - ⅓ cup water
      - 2 tsp sugar
      - ½ tsp kosher salt
  - section: Za'atar–sumac vinaigrette
    items:
      - 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
      - 2 tbsp fresh lemon juice
      - 1 tbsp za'atar
      - 1 tsp sumac
      - 1 tsp pomegranate molasses
      - 1 small clove garlic, grated
      - ¼ tsp kosher salt
  - section: Salad
    items:
      - 4 cups arugula
      - 1 cup cherry tomatoes, halved
      - 1 English cucumber, quartered lengthwise and sliced
      - 3 scallions, thinly sliced
      - ⅓ cup crumbled feta
      - ¼ cup pitted kalamata olives, halved
      - ¼ cup chopped fresh parsley
      - 2 tbsp chopped fresh mint
  - section: Sumac yogurt (optional drizzle)
    items:
      - ⅓ cup plain Greek yogurt
      - 1 tbsp fresh lemon juice
      - ½ tsp sumac
      - Pinch of kosher salt
steps:
  - section: Pita chips
    items:
      - Heat the oven to 400°F. Toss the torn pita with the olive oil, sumac, and salt until evenly coated, then spread in a single layer on a sheet pan.
      - Bake 10–12 minutes, tossing once, until deep golden and crisp all the way through. Cool on the pan — they crisp further as they sit.
  - section: Pickle the radishes
    items:
      - Warm the vinegar, water, sugar, and salt just until the sugar dissolves (a few seconds in the microwave is fine). Pour over the sliced radishes and let them sit at least 10 minutes, stirring once; drain before serving.
  - section: Dressing & sumac yogurt
    items:
      - Whisk or jar-shake the vinaigrette ingredients until emulsified. Taste and adjust — it should be bright and a little tart.
      - If using, stir together the Greek yogurt, lemon juice, sumac, and salt; loosen with a splash of water to a drizzling consistency.
  - section: Assemble
    items:
      - In a wide bowl, combine the arugula, tomatoes, cucumber, scallions, parsley, and mint. Add most of the drained pickled radishes and about three-quarters of the pita.
      - Pour the dressing around the edges and toss well, so the pita starts to soak up just enough. Add the feta and olives and toss once more, gently.
      - Pile onto plates, top with the remaining pita and radishes, and drizzle with the sumac yogurt. Finish with an extra pinch of sumac and serve right away.
tips:
  - Dress and toss the moment before serving — fattoush is meant to eat with the pita still crackling, so hold back a handful of chips to scatter on top.
  - No pomegranate molasses? Simmer ¼ cup pomegranate juice with ½ tsp sugar until syrupy, or stand in with a little more lemon plus a pinch of sugar.
  - Salt the sliced cucumber and tomatoes for 10 minutes and pat dry if you want the salad to stay crisp longer — it pulls out the water that would otherwise dilute the dressing.
  - Toast the pita a shade darker than looks right; it softens once dressed, and a deep bake keeps the crunch.
extras:
  - label: From the garden
    note: Fresh mint and parsley are the backbone here — pull them straight from the bed, and toss in a handful of arugula if it's growing. A little chopped garden cucumber never hurts.
  - label: Make it a meal
    note: Add a scoop of chickpeas or a sliced grilled chicken breast for protein, or serve alongside grilled halloumi to keep it vegetarian.
  - label: Make-ahead
    note: The pickled radishes keep a week in the fridge and the dressing holds for days; bake the pita chips the morning of. Assemble and dress only at the table.

Fattoush is the Levantine answer to "what do we do with day-old pita" — and Za'atar
in San Francisco turns it into the best thing on the table. The move that makes it
theirs is the quick-pickled radish: sliced thin, dunked in vinegar while the pita
bakes, and scattered on hot-pink at the end so every few bites lands sharp and cold
against the crunch.
