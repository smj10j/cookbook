  Crackling skin is the headline here, and it comes from one rule: dry fish, hot
  pan, don't touch. The fillets sear skin-side down until the surface shatters like
  glass, set over a low, slow confit of cherry tomatoes, garlic, and jalapeño that
  turns jammy and sweet with a slow-building heat. A cool shaved-cucumber salad with
  rosemary, basil, and lemon zest snaps the whole plate awake. *Press the skin flat
  for the first fifteen seconds and let it ride — it releases on its own when it's
  truly crisp.*

ingredients:
  - section: Sea bass
    items:
      - 2 skin-on sea bass fillets (about 6 oz each), boneless
      - 1–2 tbsp high-quality olive oil
      - Salt and freshly ground black pepper
  - section: Tomato-jalapeño confit
    items:
      - "⅓ cup olive oil"
      - 1 pint cherry or grape tomatoes, halved
      - "1 jalapeño, minced (start with ½ if heat-sensitive)"
      - 1 clove garlic, minced
      - "½ tsp fresh thyme, chopped"
      - Pinch of salt
  - section: Herbed cucumber salad
    items:
      - 1 large cucumber, thinly sliced
      - 1 tsp fresh rosemary, chopped
      - 1 tsp fresh basil, chopped
      - Zest of 1 lemon
      - 1 tbsp white wine or champagne vinegar
      - 2 tbsp olive oil
      - "Salt and cracked pepper, to taste"
      - "Optional: thinly sliced shallot or a few raw tomato halves"
  - section: To finish
    items:
      - Flaky salt (Maldon or fleur de sel)
      - Fresh basil sprigs
steps:
  - section: Prep the fish
    items:
      - Pat the sea bass completely dry, especially the skin. Set the fillets skin-side up, uncovered, in the fridge while you make everything else — extra drying time means crispier skin.
  - section: Tomato-jalapeño confit
    items:
      - In a small skillet or saucepan, combine the olive oil, halved tomatoes, minced jalapeño, garlic, thyme, and a pinch of salt.
      - Heat on low, swirling occasionally, until the tomatoes soften, blister, and turn slightly jammy, about 20–25 minutes. Keep it gentle — low and slow protects the confit's sweetness.
  - section: Herbed cucumber salad
    items:
      - Toss the sliced cucumber with the rosemary, basil, lemon zest, vinegar, olive oil, salt, and pepper (and shallot or raw tomato if using). Don't overdress — let the herbs lead. Chill until plating.
  - section: Cook the fish
    items:
      - Heat a cast-iron or stainless skillet over medium-high with 1–2 tbsp olive oil. Season the fillets with salt and pepper. When the oil shimmers, lay them skin-side down and press firmly with a spatula for 15 seconds to stop them curling.
      - Sear skin-side down 4–5 minutes until the fish is about 70% cooked through and the skin is golden and crisp. Flip and finish flesh-side down just 1–2 minutes. Don't move it while it sears — it releases naturally when ready.
  - section: Plate
    items:
      - Spoon a generous puddle of warm tomato-jalapeño confit onto each plate. Set the fish skin-side up over the confit so the skin stays crisp.
      - Add a mound of chilled cucumber salad and finish with lemon zest, flaky salt, and a sprig of basil.
tips:
  - Skin-side up in the fridge, uncovered, for even 20 minutes wicks away surface moisture — the single biggest lever for crisp skin.
  - Press the fillets flat for the first 15 seconds only; after that, leave them be, and they'll lift cleanly when the skin is set.
  - Taste a sliver of your jalapeño first — heat varies wildly, so add it in stages and let the confit tell you when it's enough.
  - Serve immediately so the crisp skin doesn't steam against the warm confit; plate skin-up, never skin-down.
extras:
  - label: From the garden
    note: This dish was built for the garden — use your own cherry tomatoes, jalapeños, rosemary, basil, and thyme. Sub a red serrano for the jalapeño for extra heat and a pop of color.
  - label: Other firm fish
    note: Halibut, mahi mahi, or drum fillets all work; for skinless fillets, sear the presentation side hard and finish gently rather than chasing crisp skin.
  - label: Pairs with
    note: A crisp, unoaked white — Vermentino, Sauvignon Blanc, or a dry rosé — keeps pace with the bright confit and herbs.

A restaurant-style plate with very little fuss: a low, slow tomato-jalapeño confit
does its thing hands-off while you chill a herby cucumber salad, then the fish gets
its moment in a screaming pan for that signature crackling skin. Garden tomatoes
and peppers make it sing.
