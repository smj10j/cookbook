  Panzanella is a lesson in patience: you salt ripe tomatoes and let them weep,
  then catch every drop of that rosy juice and whisk it into the dressing so the
  torn, olive-oil-toasted bread drinks it up. Craggy crumbs go crisp at the edges

  hot pan until the skin shatters. *The move is the tomato water — it's the whole
  flavor of summer wrung into the bowl, and stale bread is the sponge that catches
  it.* Let the salad sit ten minutes before serving so the bread turns silky but
  keeps its bite.

ingredients:
  - section: Salmon
    items:
      - 2 salmon fillets (about 6 oz each), skin-on
      - Kosher salt and freshly ground black pepper
      - 1 tbsp olive oil, for searing
  - section: Panzanella
    items:
      - 5 oz day-old sourdough or ciabatta, torn into 1-inch pieces
      - 2 tbsp olive oil, for toasting the bread
      - 1 lb ripe tomatoes (heirloom and cherry), cut into big chunks
      - ½ tsp kosher salt, for the tomatoes
      - ½ large cucumber, seeded and cut into half-moons
      - ¼ small red onion, thinly sliced
      - ¼ cup torn fresh basil leaves
      - 1 tbsp capers, drained (optional)
  - section: Tomato-water dressing
    items:
      - 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
      - 2 tbsp red wine vinegar
      - 1 clove garlic, minced or grated
      - 1 tsp Dijon mustard
      - Pinch of salt and freshly ground black pepper
steps:
  - section: Salt the tomatoes
    items:
      - Cut the tomatoes into big chunks, toss with the ½ tsp salt in a colander set over a bowl, and let them drain 15–20 minutes. Save every drop of the juice that collects — that's the base of the dressing.
  - section: Toast the bread
    items:
      - Toss the torn bread with 2 tbsp olive oil and a pinch of salt. Toast in a dry skillet over medium heat (or on a sheet pan at 400°F) 8–10 minutes, tossing, until deep gold and crisp at the edges but still chewy inside. Set aside.
  - section: Sear the salmon
    items:
      - Pat the fillets bone-dry and season both sides with salt and pepper. Heat 1 tbsp oil in a cast-iron skillet over medium-high until it shimmers. Lay the salmon in skin-side down and press gently for 20 seconds so it stays flat.
      - Sear undisturbed 4–5 minutes until the skin is crackly and releases on its own, then flip and cook 1–2 minutes more to 125–130°F. Rest on a plate.
  - section: Dress & assemble
    items:
      - Whisk the reserved tomato juice with the vinegar, garlic, Dijon, remaining olive oil, salt, and pepper until emulsified.
      - In a big bowl, combine the toasted bread, drained tomatoes, cucumber, red onion, capers, and most of the basil. Pour over the dressing and toss well. Let it sit 10 minutes so the bread drinks the juices.
      - Pile the panzanella onto two plates, set a salmon fillet on each (skin-side up to keep it crisp), and finish with the last of the basil and a grind of pepper.
tips:
  - Day-old bread is non-negotiable — fresh bread turns to mush. If yours is fresh, tear it and dry it in a 300°F oven for 10 minutes first.
  - Salt the tomatoes and hoard the juice; that rosy tomato water is the soul of the dressing and where all the summer flavor lives.
  - For shattering skin, get the fillet bone-dry, use a hot pan, and don't move it until it releases on its own — chasing it early is how the skin tears.
  - Dress the salad and wait 10 minutes, but no longer than 30 — you want the bread juicy with a little chew, not a soggy sponge.

Panzanella earns its keep on exactly the kind of night when the tomatoes are too
good to cook and the bread is a day past its best. Crisping a skin-on salmon
fillet over the top turns the Tuscan bread salad into a full dinner for two —
crackly fish, juicy bread, and a dressing built from the tomatoes' own juice.
