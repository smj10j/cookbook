title: Miso-Glazed Eggplant with Sesame Rice (Nasu Dengaku)

pitch: >-
  Nasu dengaku is proof that a broiler is a lacquer machine. Halved eggplants get
  a deep crosshatch, a slick of oil, and a slow broil until the flesh turns
  custard; then a miso-mirin glaze goes on and bubbles into a glossy, burnished
  amber shell. Sesame rice underneath catches every drip. *The crosshatch is the
  whole trick — it lets the heat melt the eggplant from the inside and gives the
  glaze somewhere to sink.*

ingredients:
  - section: Eggplant & glaze
    items:
      - 12 oz Japanese eggplant (about 2 slender ones)
      - 1 tbsp neutral oil
      - 1 tbsp white miso
      - 1 tbsp mirin
      - 2 tsp sake (or water)
      - 1½ tsp sugar
      - 1 tsp toasted sesame oil
  - section: Sesame rice
    items:
      - ½ cup short-grain rice
      - 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds
      - 2 scallions, thinly sliced
steps:
  - Rinse the rice until the water runs clear, then cook it by your usual method and keep it warm.
  - Halve the eggplants lengthwise (trim to fit the pan if needed) and score the cut faces in a deep ½-inch crosshatch, stopping just shy of the skin.
  - Brush the cut faces with the neutral oil and broil, cut side up, 8–10 minutes until deeply golden and tender all the way through.
  - Meanwhile whisk the miso, mirin, sake, sugar, and sesame oil into a smooth glaze.
  - Spoon the glaze over the eggplant and broil 1–2 minutes more, watching constantly, until it bubbles and caramelizes at the edges.
  - Serve over the rice, scattered with sesame seeds and scallions.
tips:
  - Score deeper than feels safe — a shallow crosshatch steams; a deep one turns the center to custard.
  - The glaze goes from lacquered to scorched in under a minute; park yourself at the oven door for the second broil.
  - White (shiro) miso keeps the glaze gentle and sweet; red miso works but halve it, as it is saltier and sharper.
  - No sake on hand — a splash of water loosens the glaze just as well.
