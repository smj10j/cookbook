title: Vietnamese Shaking Beef (Bò Lúc Lắc)
slug: vietnamese-shaking-beef
tagline: Seared cubes of garlicky beef tossed over watercress with a bright lime-pepper dip.
pitch: >-
  The name is the technique: bò lúc lắc means "shaking beef," for the way you
  toss the pan so every cube of marinated tenderloin catches the screaming-hot
  surface and builds a dark, caramelized crust in under two minutes. Work in a
  single layer over the highest heat you've got, finish with a swirl of butter
  and garlic, and pile the beef over peppery watercress that wilts just slightly
  under the heat. Tomato and quick-pickled red onion cut the richness, and a
  little dish of lime, salt, and black pepper — *muối tiêu chanh* — is the
  whole table's secret weapon.
  *The move: get the pan ripping hot and don't crowd it — you want a hard sear,
  not a gray steam.*
serves: 2
times:
  prep: 25
  cook: 8
  total: 45
difficulty: medium
protein: beef
methods:

cuisine: Vietnamese
course: main
heat: mild
equipment:
  - cast iron or wok
tags:
  - date-night
  - quick

ingredients:
  - section: Beef & marinade
    items:
      - 1 lb beef tenderloin (or top sirloin), cut into 1-inch cubes
      - 1 tbsp soy sauce
      - 1 tbsp oyster sauce
      - 1 tsp fish sauce
      - 2 tsp granulated sugar
      - 3 cloves garlic, minced
      - 1 tsp toasted sesame oil
      - ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
  - section: Stir-fry
    items:
      - 2 tbsp neutral oil (for searing)
      - 1 tbsp butter
      - ½ red onion, cut into thin wedges
      - 2 cloves garlic, minced
  - section: Salad bed
    items:
      - 4 cups watercress (or mixed greens)
      - 1 cup cherry tomatoes, halved (or 1 ripe tomato, wedged)
      - ½ red onion, very thinly sliced
      - 1 tbsp rice vinegar (to quick-pickle the onion)
  - section: Lime-pepper dip (muối tiêu chanh)
    items:
      - 1 lime, juiced (plus wedges to serve)
      - ½ tsp kosher salt
      - ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
steps:
  - section: Marinate
    items:
      - Pat the beef cubes very dry. Toss with the soy sauce, oyster sauce, fish sauce, sugar, garlic, sesame oil, and pepper. Let sit 20–30 minutes at room temperature (or up to 2 hours chilled).
      - Meanwhile, toss the thinly sliced red onion with the rice vinegar and a pinch of salt; let it quick-pickle while you cook. Whisk the lime juice, salt, and pepper in a small dish for the dip.
  - section: Sear
    items:
      - Lift the beef from the marinade and pat dry again (wet beef steams). Heat a cast-iron pan or wok over the highest heat until smoking, then add the neutral oil.
      - Add the beef in a single, uncrowded layer — work in two batches if needed. Let it sear undisturbed 60–90 seconds, then shake the pan (or toss) to roll the cubes and sear the other faces, about 1 minute more, until deeply browned but still pink inside.
      - Add the onion wedges, minced garlic, and butter in the last 30 seconds, shaking constantly so the garlic toasts without burning. Pull the pan the moment the beef is medium-rare; carryover finishes it.
  - section: Plate
    items:
      - Bed a platter with the watercress and tomatoes. Drain the pickled onion and scatter it over.
      - Pile the hot beef and pan juices over the greens so they wilt slightly. Serve with the lime-pepper dip and lime wedges, plus steamed jasmine rice.
tips:
  - Dry beef is the whole game — pat it twice (after cubing and again out of the marinade). Surface moisture is what keeps you from a hard sear.
  - Don't crowd the pan. Two smaller batches over max heat beat one crowded batch that steams gray. Get the pan smoking before the oil goes in.
  - Tenderloin is classic and ultra-tender, but top sirloin has more beefy flavor for less money — either works. Cut against the grain into even cubes so they cook at the same rate.
  - "Squeeze a lime wedge into the dip right before eating — the salt-pepper-lime hits brighter when the juice is fresh."
extras:
  - label: From the garden
    note: Swap in arugula for the watercress for the same peppery bite, and add torn cilantro or mint from the garden over the top.
  - label: Make it a spread
    note: Serve with a fried egg and rice for cơm bò lúc lắc, or pile into lettuce cups for a hand-held version.
  - label: Pairs with
    note: A juicy red — Côtes du Rhône or a Loire Cabernet Franc — or an ice-cold lager to match the high-heat sear.

A Vietnamese steakhouse classic that comes together faster than the rice cooker:
the marinade does the flavor work, and the only real skill is heat management —
get the pan hot enough that the beef sears instead of stews, and *shake* it like
you mean it.
