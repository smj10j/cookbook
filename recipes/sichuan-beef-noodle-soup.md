pitch: >-
  This is the bowl you crave for years after the first one. Beef browns hard in the
  Instant Pot, then pressure-cooks into a doubanjiang broth bloomed with Sichuan
  peppercorn, star anise, and dried chiles until the meat falls apart and the liquid

  blanched bok choy bowl by bowl for days. *The defining move is toasting the
  doubanjiang until its oil stains the pot red — that bloom is the whole mala
  backbone, so don't rush it.*

ingredients:
  - section: Beef
    items:
      - 3 lb beef (ideally 1.5 lb short ribs plus 1.5 lb beef shank, or 3 lb chuck)
      - Fine sea salt, to season
      - 2 tbsp neutral oil, for searing
  - section: Aromatics & vegetables
    items:
      - 1 large onion, quartered
      - 10–12 cloves garlic, smashed
      - 3–4 inch piece ginger, sliced into ¼-inch coins
      - 6 scallions (whites for broth, greens sliced for garnish)
      - 1 large carrot, cut into thick coins
  - section: Spices
    items:
      - 1½ tsp whole Sichuan peppercorns
      - 2 star anise
      - 1½ tsp dried chile flakes (or 6–10 dried chiles)
      - 2 bay leaves
      - "½ tsp fennel seeds (optional)"
  - section: Seasonings
    items:
      - ¼ cup doubanjiang (Pixian chile bean paste)
      - 3 tbsp Shaoxing wine
      - 2 tbsp dark soy sauce
      - 2–3 tsp sugar (or 1½ tsp rock sugar)
  - section: Liquid
    items:
      - 6 cups low-sodium vegetable broth
      - 2 cups water (enough to fill the pot about ⅔ full)
  - section: To serve (per bowl)
    items:
      - 24 oz dried wheat noodles (or 2 lb fresh)
      - 1–2 lb baby bok choy, quartered lengthwise
      - "½–1 tsp white pepper, for finishing"
      - 2–3 tsp toasted sesame oil, for drizzling
      - "Table-side: chili oil, Chinkiang black vinegar, pickled mustard greens, extra scallion greens"
steps:
  - section: Sear the beef
    items:
      - Pat the beef dry and season all over with fine sea salt. Set the Instant Pot to Sauté (High) and add the oil.
      - Brown the beef in batches, 3–5 minutes per side, until deeply browned. Crowding steams the meat, so give it room. Transfer to a plate.
  - section: Bloom the base
    items:
      - Into the hot pot add the scallion whites, ginger, and garlic; stir about 1 minute until fragrant.
      - Add the Sichuan peppercorns, chile flakes, star anise, bay leaves, and optional fennel; stir 1 minute to bloom.
      - Add the doubanjiang and stir until it darkens and its oil turns the pot red, 1–2 minutes. Stir in the Shaoxing wine, dark soy, and sugar.
  - section: Pressure cook
    items:
      - Return the beef and any juices to the pot. Add the carrot coins, then the vegetable broth and water until the pot is about ⅔ full. Stir to dissolve the doubanjiang.
      - Lock the lid and pressure-cook on High for 55 minutes, then let the pressure release naturally for 20 minutes.
  - section: Strain & separate
    items:
      - Lift out the beef and dunk it briefly in cool water for about a minute so the surface doesn't dry out, then set on a plate to cool.
      - Skim most of the fat from the broth, then strain through a fine-mesh strainer into a clean pot. Discard the spices and aromatics.
      - Remove any bones and slice the beef across the grain into ¼-inch slices, or tear the shank/chuck into ¾–1 inch chunks. At this point the broth and beef can be stored separately up to 5 days.
  - section: Build the bowls
    items:
      - Boil the noodles to tender-chewy, drain, and rinse briefly to keep them from clumping. Divide among bowls.
      - Return the strained broth to a simmer with the sliced beef; taste and adjust with salt, a splash more soy, or a teaspoon of sugar to round it out. Add the bok choy to the broth and cook about 1 minute until just tender.
      - Ladle broth, beef, and bok choy over the noodles. Finish with scallion greens, a pinch of white pepper, and a drizzle of sesame oil. Let each eater tune their bowl with chili oil, black vinegar, and pickled mustard greens.
tips:
  - Toasting the doubanjiang until its oil runs red is the single most important step — that bloom carries the entire mala flavor.
  - Store the beef and broth separately, and never store cooked noodles in the soup; cook fresh noodles and blanch bok choy each time so leftovers taste restaurant-fresh.
  - Adjust the mala heat at the table with chili oil rather than the pot, so the base broth stays balanced for everyone.
  - For Day 2 bowls, a fresh pinch of toasted, ground Sichuan peppercorn restores the numbing tingle the spices lose in storage.
extras:
  - label: From the garden
    note: A handful of garden cilantro and thinly sliced serrano on top brighten the rich broth; swap bok choy for any tender Chinese greens you have on hand.
  - label: Leftover strategy
    note: Freeze broth in 1–2 cup containers or silicone cubes and freeze the beef separately; both keep about 3 months. Reheat broth and beef together to a strong simmer, then add fresh noodles and greens.
  - label: Pairs with
    note: An off-dry Junmai sake or a nigori cuts the chili heat — sweet enough to cool the mala without going dessert-sweet.

servings and engineered for leftovers. Cook the beef and broth together, store them
apart, and assemble each bowl to order with fresh noodles, greens, and a table-side
mala station.
