title: Grilled Snapper with Charred Pineapple-Jalapeño Salsa
slug: grilled-snapper-pineapple-jalapeno-salsa
tagline: Blistered snapper under a smoky-sweet pineapple salsa with garden jalapeño heat.
pitch: >-
  The salsa gets grilled before the fish does — pineapple planks go over the
  hottest part of the fire until their edges blacken and the sugars turn to
  caramel, then get chopped with jalapeño, red onion, lime, and cilantro. The
  snapper follows, skin down on a clean oiled grate until it crisps and
  releases on its own. *Charred pineapple is the seasoning here — its
  bittersweet smoke does what a heavy spice rub usually would.*
serves: 2
times:
  prep: 15
  cook: 12
  total: 27
difficulty: medium
protein: fish
methods:
  - grill
cuisine: Mexican
course: main
heat: medium
equipment:
  - grill
tags:
  - summer
  - garden
  - gluten-free
ingredients:
  - section: Salsa
    items:
      - ¾ cup fresh pineapple, in thick planks
      - 1 jalapeño, seeded and minced
      - ¼ small red onion, finely diced
      - 2 tbsp cilantro, chopped
      - Juice of 1 lime
      - Pinch of salt
  - section: Snapper
    items:
      - 8 oz snapper fillets, skin on
      - 1 tbsp olive oil
      - ½ tsp ground cumin
      - ¼ tsp kosher salt
      - Freshly ground black pepper
steps:
  - Heat the grill to high and scrub the grate clean — a dirty grate is why fish sticks.
  - Grill the pineapple planks 2–3 minutes a side until well charred; dice and toss with the jalapeño, onion, cilantro, lime, and the pinch of salt.
  - Pat the fillets very dry; rub with the oil, cumin, salt, and pepper.
  - Oil the grate, lay the fish skin side down over direct heat, and leave it alone 3–4 minutes until the skin releases without a fight; flip for 1–2 minutes to finish.
  - Plate skin side up and spoon the warm salsa over.
tips:
  - If the fish resists the flip, it is not ready — give it 30 more seconds and it will let go.
  - A fish spatula (thin, flexible, long) earns its keep here.
  - Make the salsa first; it only improves while the fish cooks.
  - Swapping in mahi-mahi? Brine it, skip the skin, and pull at 130–135°F rather than judging by flake — it goes cottony past 140°F.
extras:
  - label: From the garden
    note: The jalapeño and cilantro both come from the bed — swap in serrano when you want it hotter.
  - label: Make it a bowl
    note: Serve over cilantro-lime rice with shredded cabbage for crunch.
  - label: Pairs with
    note: A cold Mexican lager with lime, or agua fresca.
  - label: Swap in mahi-mahi
    note: >-
      Mahi runs thicker, leaner, and denser than snapper, so a few things
      change. Remove the skin (mahi's is tough), then brine 15 minutes in a
      5% salt solution (50g salt per liter water) for moisture insurance, pat
      bone-dry, and oil the fish itself, not just the grates. Sear flesh side
      down over medium-high, 4–5 minutes for a 1" fillet, and don't move it —
      mahi's density makes it stick if you rush the crust; flip to finish.
      Judge doneness by firmness and a thermometer, not flake: pull at
      130–135°F, since it goes from translucent to cottony fast past 140°F.
      Its mild sweetness loves the char even more than snapper does — a
      squeeze of lime and a pinch of chile into the salsa right at the end
      cuts through the denser flesh nicely. Zero-risk option: sous vide at
      130°F for 30–40 minutes, then sear just 45 seconds a side for char.
