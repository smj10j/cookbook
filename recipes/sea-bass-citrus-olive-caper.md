pitch: >-
  This is a restaurant trick disguised as a weeknight dinner: preheat the broiler pan
  screaming hot so the fish sears the instant it lands, then broil it just six minutes
  to silky, just-set flesh. While it cooks, you stir together a no-cook sauce of halved
  lemon segments, fresh oregano, briny capers, and chopped Kalamata olives slicked with
  olive oil. Spoon it over the hot fillets and the citrus juice and brine wake the whole
  plate up.
  *Heating the pan empty first is the secret — the fish gets a hard sear on contact, no
  flipping required.*

ingredients:
  - 2 sea bass fillets (about 5 oz each), skin on
  - 1 tbsp olive oil, divided
  - ½ tsp salt, divided
  - ¼ tsp freshly ground black pepper, divided
  - 1 lemon, peeled and thinly sliced, segments halved
  - Juice of ½ lemon
  - 1 tbsp chopped fresh oregano
  - 1½ tsp capers, rinsed
  - 3 tbsp pitted Kalamata olives, roughly chopped
steps:
  - Position the broiler pan as close to the heating element as possible and heat it for 5 minutes.
  - Meanwhile, coat the fillets on both sides with about 1 tsp of the oil on a plate.
  - Carefully pull the hot pan out and set it on the stovetop. Arrange the fillets skin-side down and season with half the salt and pepper. Broil for 6 minutes.
  - In a bowl, combine the lemon slices, lemon juice, oregano, capers, olives, the remaining oil, and the remaining salt and pepper.
  - Transfer the fish to a platter and spoon the citrus-olive-caper sauce over the top.
tips:
  - Black sea bass, branzino, or any firm white fish all work beautifully — keep the skin on so it crisps under the broiler.
  - Make the sauce first so the flavors meld while the pan heats and the fish cooks.
  - No need to flip — a properly preheated pan sears the underside while the broiler cooks the top through.

Liza Schoenfein's quick broiler method, scaled for two: preheat the pan blazing hot,
broil the fish six minutes, and finish with a raw sauce of lemon, oregano, capers, and
Kalamata. Bright, briny, and on the table in 25 minutes.
