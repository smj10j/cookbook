title: Tilapia Piccata with Fettuccine & Broccoli
slug: tilapia-piccata-fettuccine-broccoli
tagline: Crisp-seared tilapia in a glossy lemon-caper pan sauce over fettuccine and charred broccoli.
pitch: >-
  This is chicken piccata's lighter, faster cousin — delicate tilapia gets a
  whisper-thin flour dredge so it crisps golden without going gummy, leaving a
  fond that becomes the backbone of the sauce. Deglaze with white wine, simmer
  down lemon juice and briny capers, then swirl in cold butter off the heat until
  the sauce goes glossy and clings. The same skillet chars broccoli florets so
  they drink up the drippings, and the fettuccine finishes right in the pan with a
  splash of starchy water. *The trick is the off-heat butter swirl — monter au
  beurre — that turns sharp lemon and wine into a silky emulsion that coats every
  noodle.*

ingredients:
  - section: Tilapia
    items:
      - 2 tilapia fillets (5–8 oz each)
      - Kosher salt and freshly cracked black pepper
      - ¼ cup all-purpose flour (for a micro-dredge)
      - 2 tbsp olive oil
      - 1 tbsp unsalted butter (for searing)
  - section: Piccata sauce
    items:
      - 2–3 cloves garlic, minced
      - ½ cup dry white wine (Sauvignon Blanc or Pinot Grigio), or low-sodium chicken stock
      - 3–4 thin lemon slices
      - Juice of 1 large lemon (~3 tbsp)
      - 2 tbsp capers, drained (smash a few lightly)
      - 2 tbsp unsalted butter (for finishing)
      - 2 tbsp fresh parsley, finely chopped
  - section: Broccoli & pasta
    items:
      - 1 large head broccoli, cut into florets (peel and slice the stem)
      - 6 oz fettuccine
      - Olive oil, salt, and pepper
      - Pinch of chili flakes (optional)
      - ½ cup reserved pasta water
steps:
  - section: Prep
    items:
      - Pat the tilapia completely dry, season both sides with salt and pepper, then dredge lightly in flour and shake off the excess — a thin coat means a crisp crust and a clean, glossy sauce.
      - Cut the broccoli into florets; peel the stem and slice it thin to sear alongside. Toss with olive oil, salt, pepper, and chili flakes if using.
      - Mince the garlic, slice the lemon into thin wheels, juice the remaining lemon, chop the parsley, and drain the capers (smash a few with the flat of your knife for extra brine).
  - section: Pasta & broccoli
    items:
      - Bring a large pot of well-salted water to a boil and cook the fettuccine to al dente. Reserve ½ cup pasta water, then drain and toss with a little olive oil to keep it loose.
      - Meanwhile, heat a large skillet over medium-high. Add a drizzle of oil and the broccoli in a single layer; leave it untouched 2–3 minutes to char, toss once, sear 1 minute more, then scoot it to the side of the pan or remove to a bowl.
  - section: Sear & sauce
    items:
      - Add the 2 tbsp olive oil and 1 tbsp butter to the cleared pan. Lay in the tilapia and sear undisturbed about 3 minutes until golden, flip, and cook 2–3 minutes more until it just flakes. Transfer to a warm plate.
      - Lower the heat to medium. Add the garlic and cook 20–30 seconds until fragrant, then lay in the lemon slices and let them caramelize 30–45 seconds per side.
      - Pour in the wine (or stock) and scrape up the fond. Add the capers and reduce by about half, 1–2 minutes. Stir in the lemon juice.
      - Off the heat, swirl in the 2 tbsp finishing butter a pat at a time until the sauce turns glossy, then stir in the parsley. Taste and adjust salt and acid.
  - section: Finish
    items:
      - Add the drained fettuccine and broccoli to the sauce, splashing in pasta water as needed until everything is silky and coated.
      - Nest the pasta and broccoli into shallow bowls, lay the tilapia on top, and spoon the remaining sauce over the fish.
tips:
  - Pull the tilapia the instant it flakes — it is delicate, and carryover heat finishes it on the plate. Overcooked, it turns chalky.
  - Searing the lemon slices caramelizes their acidity into a deeper, rounder citrus flavor; don't skip them in the sauce.
  - Reserve more pasta water than you think you need — its starch is what makes the butter-wine sauce cling to the noodles instead of pooling.
  - If the sauce tastes too sharp at the end, swirl in one more pat of cold butter; too flat, add a final squeeze of lemon.
extras:
  - label: From the garden
    note: Finish with extra chopped parsley and a little fresh thyme, and brighten the sauce with garden lemon zest grated in just before serving.
  - label: Pairs with
    note: A crisp, unoaked white — Sauvignon Blanc or Pinot Grigio — echoes the wine in the sauce. Add grilled bread to mop up the extra.
  - label: Make it greener
    note: Swap or supplement the broccoli with broccolini or asparagus tips, charred the same way in the skillet.
